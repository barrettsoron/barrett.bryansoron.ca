An argument is circulating in the coalitions working to stop data centre expansion: using any AI tool, even a language model to help process a permit application, is a moral betrayal of the fight. The logic seems clean. You can't fight the expansion of AI infrastructure while using AI. You're funding the thing you're fighting.

This is wrong, and the wrongness is practical: accept it, and you have handed the adversary a free pass while feeling principled about it.

### Taking Lorde seriously

The most serious version of this argument invokes Audre Lorde: [*the master's tools will never dismantle the master's house*](https://monoskop.org/images/2/2b/Lorde_Audre_1983_The_Masters_Tools_Will_Never_Dismantle_the_Masters_House.pdf). It's the right starting point. Lorde was making a substantive argument, and it deserves engagement on its own terms.

The piece — originally a comment delivered at the 1979 Second Sex Conference, later collected in *Sister Outsider* — was addressed to white academic feminists who had organized a conference without centring Black women or lesbians, who had reproduced inside their own movement the exclusions of the dominant culture they claimed to challenge. The "master's tools" were *epistemological* tools: the categories, the methods, the frameworks that build in certain exclusions and cannot see past them. Her argument was about method and consciousness within a movement: you cannot think your way to liberation using the thought-patterns of the people doing the oppressing.

That is a serious argument about epistemology and method. It addresses what a movement does to its own members' capacity for analysis. It says nothing about which software an organizer reaches for.

Collapsing Lorde's point into "never use technology made by capital" rules out laptops, encrypted messaging, and the electrical grid powering every organizer's phone. It also misses her insight: the warning is about consciousness, about what your practice teaches you to see. Using AI to process a 200-page environmental impact assessment is instrumental. It carries no commitment to Sam Altman's vision of the world.

Lorde's caution, properly applied, is about this: adopt the AI companies' framing that scale is inherently good, that efficiency is the highest value, that data centres are neutral infrastructure requiring no democratic consent, and you have lost before you started. That warning is real. Deliberate, bounded use is the answer to it.

Lorde's deeper warning is about practice shaping consciousness — that what you do habitually changes what you can see. That warning is real here too. The answer to it is discipline: use AI for defined tasks, keep the analysis with your people, and you have not handed your political judgment to the platform.

A campaign that refuses to use a language model to read environmental assessments, while Big Tech's lawyers use AI to generate those assessments, has not taken the moral high ground. It has taken the practical low ground.

### The mirror world and what it cost

Naomi Klein, writing in [*Doppelganger*](https://www.booksellers.ca/books/doppelganger-naomi-klein-9781039006898.html?a=2370) (2023), documents how the conspiratorial right built an alternative media infrastructure: podcasts, YouTube channels, newsletters. The left either refused to engage on principle or engaged badly. The tools were not inherently conservative. The right was simply willing to use them.

The 2010s were the dress rehearsal. Cambridge Analytica used Facebook's own data infrastructure to surgically target swing voters. Breitbart built a Facebook-native media ecosystem optimized for algorithmic amplification. The left was running email lists and town halls. Digital asymmetry was load-bearing in everything that followed: Brexit, Trump's first election, the AfD's electoral gains, the Rassemblement National's breakthrough in France.

Zeynep Tufekci, in *Twitter and Tear Gas* (2017), showed how social media organizing could mobilize fast but built little durable capacity: speed purchased at the cost of depth. The discipline question and the use question are separate. Collapsing them produces a false clarity: you feel principled, and you have answered neither.

### The infrastructure we are fighting

What we are fighting, concretely, is the infrastructure: the energy draw from Hydro-Québec and BC Hydro; the water consumption in drought-stressed Alberta communities; the land seizures on unceded territory, conducted without free, prior, and informed consent; the US CLOUD Act's reach into any data stored on servers owned by US companies; the Carney government's AIDA freeze and the regulatory retreat it represents. [Paris Marx](https://thetyee.ca/Opinion/2026/04/16/Every-Data-Centre-US-Military-Base/) names the pattern directly: Canadian capital choosing to join the digital colonizers rather than dismantle colonization.

Kate Crawford's *Atlas of AI* (2021) locates AI's harms precisely: in the supply chain, the mineral extraction, the labour exploitation, the energy consumption, the concentration of ownership and deployment at scale. Shoshana Zuboff adds a second coordinate: "behavioural surplus," data harvested beyond service delivery and sold to prediction markets. The fight against data centres is a fight against both of those logics of extraction.

Summarizing a land-use permit application with a language model uses existing compute for a defined task. The harm Crawford and Zuboff name lives in the supply chain, the build-out, the concentration of ownership at scale, the systematic harvesting of behaviour. A defined research task sits on a different map entirely. Conflating "AI as extractive infrastructure" with "AI as tool" is a category error that costs us operational effectiveness while buying nothing.

### What Doctorow calls comcom

There is a name for using the adversary's infrastructure against them: adversarial competitive compatibility (comcom). It's the exemplary Canadian Cory Doctorow's term, from [his writing on interoperability](https://spectrum.ieee.org/doctorow-interoperability). You extract value from a system you are fighting, against that system's interests, without endorsing it.

The civil rights movement used federal courts, a white supremacist institution, to dismantle legal segregation. The labour movement used mass-circulation newspapers owned by hostile capital to organize. The anti-apartheid movement used SWIFT and international capital markets, the infrastructure of the economic order they opposed, to coordinate sanctions against that order.

[David Moscrop](https://jacobin.com/2025/02/artificial-intelligence-workers-ownership-tech)'s framing applies directly: robots won't set us free unless we control the robots. The question is who controls powerful tools, and toward what end. Movements that use tools deliberately, with discipline, while fighting for democratic control of those tools have a better record than movements that refuse available tools on purity grounds.

### The sovereignty ladder

Clean hands are unavailable inside a capitalist economy. Every campaign laptop runs on the same grid as a data centre. Signal runs on iOS and Android, both corporate surveillance platforms. The phones used for encrypted messaging came from lithium supply chains involving extraction and exploitation. This is the condition of organizing here, at this time.

The operative question, one I [worked through in detail during the Lewis campaign](https://barrett.bryansoron.ca/essays/notes-from-the-lewis-for-leader-campaign-on-digital-sovereignty/), is deliberateness: what am I choosing, and toward what end.

Deliberate, bounded use with discipline is sovereignty practice. Know what data you're exposing. Build only the dependencies you can unwind. Use AI for defined tasks: document processing, permit research, draft language. Keep the organizational intelligence in your people.

Using a language model as a research assistant, with your membership data and organizing strategy kept off the platform, is adversarial use of the adversary's infrastructure.

That is comcom in practice. That is how we win.
