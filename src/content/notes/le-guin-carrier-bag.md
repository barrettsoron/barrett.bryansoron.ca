Rereading Ursula K. Le Guin's *The Carrier Bag Theory of Fiction* (1986).

Her argument, compressed: the dominant shape of Western storytelling is the spear — a protagonist, a conflict, a killing, a return. But the older and more human technology, she says, is the bag. The basket. The sling. The container that lets us carry home what we gathered and come back tomorrow to do it again.

> The natural, proper, fitting shape of the novel might be that of a sack, a bag. A book holds words. Words hold things. They bear meanings. A novel is a medicine bundle, holding things in a particular, powerful relation to one another and to us.

I think about this essay every time I find myself pulled toward the spear-shape — the big argument, the definitive take, the piece that *wins*. Most writing worth doing is bag-shaped. You gather things. You carry them home. You show them to people later, in an arrangement that makes sense to you.

A site like this one is a bag.
