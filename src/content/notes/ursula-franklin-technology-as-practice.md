Ursula Franklin delivered the CBC Massey Lectures in 1989. They were published as *The Real World of Technology* and remain, thirty-seven years on, the clearest short book I know about what technology is actually doing to us.

Her key move is a definition. Technology, she says, is not a set of things — machines, devices, tools. Technology is *practice*. "The way we do things around here." And once you see it that way, the questions shift. You stop asking whether a given tool is good or bad and start asking what pattern of life it enforces, what skills it makes obsolete, what decisions it takes away from you.

She distinguishes two broad modes. *Holistic technologies* keep the practitioner in charge — a potter throwing a pot, a farmer reading a field. *Prescriptive technologies* break the work into stages that must be done in a specified order, by workers who don't need to understand the whole. The assembly line is the canonical example. Most software is another.

Franklin was a Quaker, a metallurgist, a pacifist, and one of the first people to call out the costs of prescriptive technology as a form of discipline — a way of organizing people around the shape of machines.

If you read one Canadian writer on technology this year, make it her.
